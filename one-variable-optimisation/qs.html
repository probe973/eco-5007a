Differentiation Practice

For each of the functions below, find the first and second derivative:

  1. $y = 5x^3 + 2x^2 - 8x + 3$

    Find $\frac{dy}{dx}$ and $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}$

    Show Solution
    $$ \frac{dy}{dx} = 15x^2 + 4x - 8 $$ $$ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 30x + 4 $$

  2. $m = 4x - 3 + \frac{2}{x} - \frac{4}{x^2}$

    Find $\frac{dm}{dx}$ and $\frac{d^2m}{dx^2}$

    Show Solution

    $m = 4x - 3 + 2x^{-1} - 4x^{-2}$

    $$ \frac{dm}{dx} = 4 - 2x^{-2} + 8x^{-3} = 4 - \frac{2}{x^2} + \frac{8}{x^3} $$ $$ \frac{d^2m}{dx^2} = 4x^{-3} - 24x^{-4} = \frac{4}{x^3} - \frac{24}{x^4} $$

  3. $f(t) = 24 + 4\sqrt{t}$

    Find $f'(t)$ and $f''(t)$

    Show Solution

    $f(t) = 24 + 4t^{1/2}$

    $$ f'(t) = 2t^{-1/2} = \frac{2}{\sqrt{t}} $$ $$ f''(t) = -t^{-3/2} = -\frac{1}{t^{3/2}} $$

  4. $P = t^3(5t^3 - 4t^{-3/2})$

    Find $\frac{dP}{dt}$ and $\frac{d^2P}{dt^2}$

    Show Solution

    $P = 5t^{6} - 4t^{3/2}$

    $$ \frac{dP}{dt} = 30t^5 - 6t^{1/2} = 30t^5 - 6\sqrt{t} $$ $$ \frac{d^2P}{dt^2} = 150t^4 - 3t^{-1/2} = 150t^4 - \frac{3}{\sqrt{t}} $$

  5. $g(x) = 3x^5 - 4x^2 + \frac{7}{x}$ (domain: $x > 0$)

    Find $\frac{dg}{dx}$ and $\frac{d^2g}{dx^2}$

    Show Solution

    $g(x) = 3x^5 - 4x^2 + 7x^{-1}$

    $$ \frac{dg}{dx} = 15x^4 - 8x - 7x^{-2} = 15x^4 - 8x - \frac{7}{x^2} $$ $$ \frac{d^2g}{dx^2} = 60x^3 - 8 + 14x^{-3} = 60x^3 - 8 + \frac{14}{x^3} $$

  6. $y = 10x^4 + 6x^{1/3} - \frac{3}{x^2}$

    Find $\frac{dy}{dx}$ and $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}$

    Show Solution

    $y = 10x^4 + 6x^{1/3} - 3x^{-2}$

    $$ \frac{dy}{dx} = 40x^3 + 2x^{-2/3} + 6x^{-3} = 40x^3 + \frac{2}{x^{2/3}} + \frac{6}{x^3} $$ $$ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 120x^2 - \frac{4}{3}x^{-5/3} - 18x^{-4} = 120x^2 - \frac{4}{3x^{5/3}} - \frac{18}{x^4} $$

Stationary Points

A point is stationary if its gradient is equal to zero, i.e. $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$.

Substituting the stationary point into the second derivative of the function determines whether the stationary point is a local minimum, local maximum, or a point of inflection:

  • $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} < 0$   $\Rightarrow$   Local maximum
  • $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} > 0$   $\Rightarrow$   Local minimum
  • $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 0$ and $\frac{d^3y}{dx^3} \neq 0$   $\Rightarrow$   Point of inflection

For each function below:

  • Find the first derivative
  • Solve where the first derivative equals zero, and find the coordinates of the stationary point(s)
  • Find the second derivative
  • Substitute result(s) into the second derivative to determine the nature of the stationary point(s)
  • Plot the functions using an online graphing tool (e.g. Desmos)

Functions:

  1. $y = x^2 + 8x - 9$

    Show Solution
    • First Derivative: $\frac{dy}{dx} = 2x + 8$
    • Stationary Point(s): Set $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$. $$ 2x + 8 = 0 \implies x = -4 $$ Substitute $x=-4$ into $y$: $$ y = (-4)^2 + 8(-4) - 9 = 16 - 32 - 9 = -25 $$ Stationary point: $(-4, -25)$.
    • Second Derivative: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 2$
    • Nature of Stationary Point(s): At $x=-4$: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 2$. Since $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 2 > 0$, the stationary point $(-4, -25)$ is a local minimum.

  2. $y = 2x^3 + 6x^2 - 90x - 2$

    Show Solution
    • First Derivative: $\frac{dy}{dx} = 6x^2 + 12x - 90$
    • Stationary Point(s): Set $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$. $$ 6x^2 + 12x - 90 = 0 $$ $$ x^2 + 2x - 15 = 0 \quad \text{(Divide by 6)} $$ $$ (x+5)(x-3) = 0 $$ $x = -5$ or $x = 3$. For $x = -5$: $y = 2(-5)^3 + 6(-5)^2 - 90(-5) - 2 = -250 + 150 + 450 - 2 = 348$. Stationary point: $(-5, 348)$. For $x = 3$: $y = 2(3)^3 + 6(3)^2 - 90(3) - 2 = 54 + 54 - 270 - 2 = -164$. Stationary point: $(3, -164)$.
    • Second Derivative: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 12x + 12$
    • Nature of Stationary Point(s): At $x = -5$: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 12(-5) + 12 = -48$. Since $-48 < 0$, $(-5, 348)$ is a local maximum. At $x = 3$: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 12(3) + 12 = 48$. Since $48 > 0$, $(3, -164)$ is a local minimum.

  3. $y = 4x^{3/2} - x^2$ (domain: $x > 0$)

    Show Solution
    • First Derivative: $\frac{dy}{dx} = 6x^{1/2} - 2x = 6\sqrt{x} - 2x$
    • Stationary Point(s): Set $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$. $$ 6\sqrt{x} - 2x = 0 \implies 2\sqrt{x}(3 - \sqrt{x}) = 0 $$ Given $x > 0$, we have $3 - \sqrt{x} = 0 \implies \sqrt{x}=3 \implies x=9$. For $x = 9$: $y = 4(9)^{3/2} - (9)^2 = 4(27) - 81 = 108 - 81 = 27$. Stationary point: $(9, 27)$.
    • Second Derivative: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 3x^{-1/2} - 2 = \frac{3}{\sqrt{x}} - 2$
    • Nature of Stationary Point(s): At $x = 9$: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{3}{\sqrt{9}} - 2 = 1 - 2 = -1$. Since $-1 < 0$, the stationary point $(9, 27)$ is a local maximum.

  4. $y = x \ln x$ (use the product rule)

    Show Solution
    • First Derivative: Using product rule ($u=x, v=\ln x$), $\frac{dy}{dx} = (1)(\ln x) + (x)(\frac{1}{x}) = \ln x + 1$
    • Stationary Point(s): Set $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$. $$ \ln x + 1 = 0 \implies \ln x = -1 \implies x = e^{-1} = \frac{1}{e} $$ For $x = \frac{1}{e}$: $y = \frac{1}{e} \ln(\frac{1}{e}) = \frac{1}{e}(-1) = -\frac{1}{e}$. Stationary point: $(\frac{1}{e}, -\frac{1}{e})$.
    • Second Derivative: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{1}{x}$
    • Nature of Stationary Point(s): At $x = \frac{1}{e}$: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{1}{1/e} = e$. Since $e > 0$, the stationary point $(\frac{1}{e}, -\frac{1}{e})$ is a local minimum.

  5. $y = 6x^{2/3} - 4x$ (domain: $x > 0$)

    Show Solution
    • First Derivative: $\frac{dy}{dx} = 4x^{-1/3} - 4 = \frac{4}{\sqrt[3]{x}} - 4$
    • Stationary Point(s): Set $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$. $$ \frac{4}{\sqrt[3]{x}} - 4 = 0 \implies \frac{4}{\sqrt[3]{x}} = 4 \implies \sqrt[3]{x} = 1 \implies x = 1 $$ For $x = 1$: $y = 6(1)^{2/3} - 4(1) = 6 - 4 = 2$. Stationary point: $(1, 2)$.
    • Second Derivative: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = -\frac{4}{3}x^{-4/3} = -\frac{4}{3x^{4/3}}$
    • Nature of Stationary Point(s): At $x = 1$: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = -\frac{4}{3(1)^{4/3}} = -\frac{4}{3}$. Since $-\frac{4}{3} < 0$, the stationary point $(1, 2)$ is a local maximum.

  6. $y = x^3 - 3x$

    Show Solution
    • First Derivative: $\frac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2 - 3$
    • Stationary Point(s): Set $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$. $$ 3x^2 - 3 = 0 \implies x^2 = 1 \implies x = \pm 1 $$ For $x = 1$: $y = (1)^3 - 3(1) = -2$. Stationary point: $(1, -2)$. For $x = -1$: $y = (-1)^3 - 3(-1) = 2$. Stationary point: $(-1, 2)$.
    • Second Derivative: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6x$
    • Nature of Stationary Point(s): At $x = 1$: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6(1) = 6$. Since $6 > 0$, $(1, -2)$ is a local minimum. At $x = -1$: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6(-1) = -6$. Since $-6 < 0$, $(-1, 2)$ is a local maximum.

  7. $y = x + \frac{3}{x} + 2$

    Show Solution
    • First Derivative: $y = x + 3x^{-1} + 2 \implies \frac{dy}{dx} = 1 - 3x^{-2} = 1 - \frac{3}{x^2}$
    • Stationary Point(s): Set $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$. $$ 1 - \frac{3}{x^2} = 0 \implies x^2 = 3 \implies x = \pm\sqrt{3} $$ For $x = \sqrt{3}$: $y = \sqrt{3} + \frac{3}{\sqrt{3}} + 2 = \sqrt{3} + \sqrt{3} + 2 = 2\sqrt{3} + 2$. Stationary point: $(\sqrt{3}, 2\sqrt{3} + 2)$. For $x = -\sqrt{3}$: $y = -\sqrt{3} + \frac{3}{-\sqrt{3}} + 2 = -\sqrt{3} - \sqrt{3} + 2 = -2\sqrt{3} + 2$. Stationary point: $(-\sqrt{3}, -2\sqrt{3} + 2)$.
    • Second Derivative: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6x^{-3} = \frac{6}{x^3}$
    • Nature of Stationary Point(s): At $x = \sqrt{3}$: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{6}{(\sqrt{3})^3} = \frac{6}{3\sqrt{3}} = \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}}$. Since $\frac{2}{\sqrt{3}} > 0$, $(\sqrt{3}, 2\sqrt{3} + 2)$ is a local minimum. At $x = -\sqrt{3}$: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{6}{(-\sqrt{3})^3} = -\frac{2}{\sqrt{3}}$. Since $-\frac{2}{\sqrt{3}} < 0$, $(-\sqrt{3}, -2\sqrt{3} + 2)$ is a local maximum.

  8. $y = x^3 - 6x^2 + 9x + 5$

    Show Solution
    • First Derivative: $\frac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2 - 12x + 9$
    • Stationary Point(s): Set $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$. $$ 3x^2 - 12x + 9 = 0 \implies x^2 - 4x + 3 = 0 \implies (x-1)(x-3) = 0 $$ $x = 1$ or $x = 3$. For $x = 1$: $y = (1)^3 - 6(1)^2 + 9(1) + 5 = 1 - 6 + 9 + 5 = 9$. Stationary point: $(1, 9)$. For $x = 3$: $y = (3)^3 - 6(3)^2 + 9(3) + 5 = 27 - 54 + 27 + 5 = 5$. Stationary point: $(3, 5)$.
    • Second Derivative: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6x - 12$
    • Nature of Stationary Point(s): At $x = 1$: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6(1) - 12 = -6$. Since $-6 < 0$, $(1, 9)$ is a local maximum. At $x = 3$: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6(3) - 12 = 6$. Since $6 > 0$, $(3, 5)$ is a local minimum.

  9. $y = x^4 - 8x^2 + 5$

    Show Solution
    • First Derivative: $\frac{dy}{dx} = 4x^3 - 16x$
    • Stationary Point(s): Set $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$. $$ 4x^3 - 16x = 0 \implies 4x(x^2 - 4) = 0 \implies 4x(x-2)(x+2) = 0 $$ $x = 0$, $x = 2$, or $x = -2$. For $x = 0$: $y = (0)^4 - 8(0)^2 + 5 = 5$. Stationary point: $(0, 5)$. For $x = 2$: $y = (2)^4 - 8(2)^2 + 5 = 16 - 32 + 5 = -11$. Stationary point: $(2, -11)$. For $x = -2$: $y = (-2)^4 - 8(-2)^2 + 5 = 16 - 32 + 5 = -11$. Stationary point: $(-2, -11)$.
    • Second Derivative: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 12x^2 - 16$
    • Nature of Stationary Point(s): At $x = 0$: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 12(0)^2 - 16 = -16$. Since $-16 < 0$, $(0, 5)$ is a local maximum. At $x = 2$: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 12(2)^2 - 16 = 32$. Since $32 > 0$, $(2, -11)$ is a local minimum. At $x = -2$: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 12(-2)^2 - 16 = 32$. Since $32 > 0$, $(-2, -11)$ is a local minimum.

Optimisation: Open-Top Box Problem

A box has a square base of dimensions $x \times x$ and height $y$. The box is open at the top.

  1. Write a formula for the surface area of the open-top box.

    Show Solution
    $$ A = (\text{Area of base}) + (\text{Area of 4 sides}) $$ $$ A = (x \cdot x) + 4(x \cdot y) $$ $$ A = x^2 + 4xy $$

  2. Suppose the surface area is fixed at 300 cm$^2$. Show that this constraint implies:

    $$ y = \frac{300}{4x} - \frac{x}{4} $$
    Show Solution

    Given $A = 300$:

    $$ \begin{aligned} 300 &= x^2 + 4xy \\ 300 - x^2 &= 4xy \\ \frac{300 - x^2}{4x} &= y \\ y &= \frac{300}{4x} - \frac{x^2}{4x} \\ y &= \frac{300}{4x} - \frac{x}{4} \end{aligned} $$

  3. Write a formula for the volume of the box in terms of $x$ and $y$.

    Show Solution
    $$ V = \text{length} \times \text{width} \times \text{height} $$ $$ V = x \cdot x \cdot y $$ $$ V = x^2y $$

  4. Substitute the expression for $y$ into the volume formula so the volume is a function of $x$ only.

    Show Solution

    Substitute $y = \frac{300}{4x} - \frac{x}{4}$ into $V = x^2y$:

    $$ \begin{aligned} V(x) &= x^2 \left( \frac{300}{4x} - \frac{x}{4} \right) \\ V(x) &= \frac{300x^2}{4x} - \frac{x^3}{4} \\ V(x) &= 75x - \frac{1}{4}x^3 \end{aligned} $$

  5. Find the value of $x$ that maximises the volume.

    Show Solution

    To maximise volume, find $\frac{dV}{dx}$ and set it to zero.

    $$ \begin{aligned} \frac{dV}{dx} &= \frac{d}{dx}\left(75x - \frac{1}{4}x^3\right) \\ &= 75 - \frac{3}{4}x^2 \end{aligned} $$

    Set $\frac{dV}{dx} = 0$:

    $$ \begin{aligned} 75 - \frac{3}{4}x^2 &= 0 \\ 75 &= \frac{3}{4}x^2 \\ 300 &= 3x^2 \\ 100 &= x^2 \\ x &= \pm 10 \end{aligned} $$

    Since $x$ is a dimension, $x > 0$, so $x = 10 \text{ cm}$.

    To confirm this is a maximum, find the second derivative $\frac{d^2V}{dx^2}$.

    $$ \begin{aligned} \frac{d^2V}{dx^2} &= \frac{d}{dx}\left(75 - \frac{3}{4}x^2\right) \\ &= -\frac{3}{2}x \end{aligned} $$

    At $x=10$:

    $$ \begin{aligned} \frac{d^2V}{dx^2}\Big|_{x=10} &= -\frac{3}{2}(10) \\ &= -15 \end{aligned} $$

    Since $\frac{d^2V}{dx^2} = -15 < 0$, the volume is maximised at $x = 10 \text{ cm}$.

  6. Calculate the corresponding height $y$ and determine the maximum volume.

    Show Solution

    Using $x=10 \text{ cm}$ and $y = \frac{300}{4x} - \frac{x}{4}$:

    $$ \begin{aligned} y &= \frac{300}{4(10)} - \frac{10}{4} \\ &= \frac{300}{40} - 2.5 \\ &= 7.5 - 2.5 \\ &= 5 \text{ cm} \end{aligned} $$

    The corresponding height is $\mathbf{5 \text{ cm}}$.

    Calculate the maximum volume using $V = x^2y$:

    $$ \begin{aligned} V_{\text{max}} &= (10 \text{ cm})^2 \cdot (5 \text{ cm}) \\ &= 100 \text{ cm}^2 \cdot 5 \text{ cm} \\ &= 500 \text{ cm}^3 \end{aligned} $$

    The maximum volume is $\mathbf{500 \text{ cm}^3}$.
